Yet despite this remarkable progress, AGI by 2030 remains improbable for three fundamental reasons that transcend hype cycles.

**The Definition Problem Obscures Real Progress**


The term "AGI" has become so elastic it's nearly meaningless. There is no universally accepted definition of the term
. 
People strongly disagree on its definition: Some define AGI by its performance on benchmarks, others by its internal workings, its economic impact, or vibes. Does the term mean software that matches the cognitive abilities of an average human? Or the abilities of the humans with the highest IQs? Or the best expert in each individual domain of knowledge? The Hendrycks and Bengio research paper, for instance, defines AGI as matching or exceeding "the cognitive versatility and proficiency of a well-educated adult." The DeepMind paper proposes measuring against a representative sample of adults
. 


ARC-AGI claims to be "the only AI benchmark that measures our progress towards general intelligence"
, while others point to economic automation thresholds. This definitional chaos matters because 
underspecification leads to cascading epistemic problems, including irrefutability, lack of external validity, flawed experimental design, and flawed evaluation. These common problems in AI research are worsened in the AGI context by the lack of scientifically grounded definitions of AGI
. 
To understand how close current models are to AGI, we must focus on where they lose points in the definitional framework. Ultimately, our framework allows us to replace vague speculation with a quantitative diagnostic
. Without consensus on what we're measuring, declaring victory becomes arbitrary.

**Benchmark Saturation Masks Shallow Understanding**

Current AI success stories reflect impressive but brittle pattern matching, not generalizable intelligence. The biggest challenge is benchmark saturation – models perform so well on existing tests that benchmarks can no longer effectively indicate capability and performance, with developers optimizing for specific benchmarks through training on the same datasets, creating 'overfits' where models perform well on test data but struggle with real-world scenarios. While benchmark saturation isn't inherently negative if models genuinely master tasks, it becomes problematic when it reflects loss of measurement resolution rather than capability completion, where 
apparent convergence may mask unresolved weaknesses in robustness, calibration, or generalization. External validity is also relevant when researchers equate human faculties with model proxies, such as claiming that a model "capable of linking specific objects with more general concepts"
 represents genuine understanding.


LLMs work as repositories of vector programs that "memorize, fetch, apply" patterns but cannot adapt to novelty or pick up new skills on the fly – there is no fluid intelligence at play here. ARC Prize focuses instead on tasks that humans solve effortlessly yet AI finds challenging which highlight fundamental gaps in AI's reasoning and adaptability. This approach reveals the essential qualities of intelligence - such as the ability to generalize from limited examples, synthesize symbolic rules, and flexibly apply known concepts in novel contexts - that current AI systems struggle to replicate
.

**Structural Deployment Barriers Create Institutional Lag**

The path from laboratory breakthrough to transformative deployment faces systemic obstacles. 
AI regulation suffers from institutional barriers; fragmentation of international law, geopolitical realities, human rights, economic considerations, and the slow decision-making process globally. Implementation faces several challenges, including divergent regulatory frameworks and safety standards that amplify the potential for catastrophic failures, whether through system misalignment or malicious use. These malfunctions could manifest in unintended behaviors, such as lying to users that a system is functioning as intended when it is malfunctioning because AI systems are pursuing goals that diverge from those intended by their developers. Such issues are further enhanced by the inability to create effective governance and regulatory structures that might manage and reduce the risk that rapidly proliferating AI might pose
.


Companies that use AI for work currently requiring rare human talent gain significant competitive advantages and will structure their activities to preserve those advantages under regulatory constraints. On a global scale, governance challenges arise from the fragmented nature of AI regulatory approaches across nations, creating barriers to international collaboration and interoperability. As AI systems become increasingly autonomous, ensuring human oversight and accountability will become more challenging in the long term. Advanced AI systems with poorly aligned goals could pose unpredictable and potentially catastrophic consequences, raising existential risks
. This creates a paradox where the most transformative systems operate in regulatory shadows, slowing economy-wide adoption.

**The Probabilistic Reality**

AGI by 2030 isn't impossible—it's just improbable given current trajectories. Expert judgment and structured surveys place the 50% probability for broadly human-level general intelligence in the 2040s, not in the immediate next one to three years, with large surveys of AI researchers showing medians in the 2040s–2050s for a 50% chance of machines outperforming humans across the board. The forecast weighted by expert tier suggests a median arrival time for transformative AI around 2035-2040, with ~25-35% probability by 2030 and meaningful probability mass extending to 2050 or beyond.

The remarkable progress from GPT-4 to o3 demonstrates genuine advances in reasoning capabilities, but the gap between solving mathematical olympiad problems and achieving general intelligence remains vast. 
True AGI will require not just better pattern matching, but fundamental breakthroughs in adaptability, common sense reasoning, and real-world deployment at scale. These significant barriers make an AGI Score of 100% unlikely in the next year. Achieving AGI requires solving a variety of grand challenges
—challenges that six years may prove insufficient to solve.
